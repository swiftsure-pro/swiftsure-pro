Typical Compliance Concerns
This guideline provides a sampling of typical compliance concerns.

Main Description

Here are some common examples of compliance management concerns:

  1. How can I manage the multi-year effort required to implement regulatory changes across all of my systems, controlling the costs, budget, delivery, functionality and integration?
    • This is a complex problem requiring portfolio management expertise integrated with program and project management
  2. How will I demonstrate a linkage between the business processes I develop and the policies that my processes satisfy?
    • Auditing specialists may look for documentation that seeks to understand how corporate policy was implemented. This may be accomplished through the use of a document, or position paper that describes a plan for achieving compliance.
  3. How will I document my existing business processes and systems, as they are implemented today?
    • Legislation like the Sarbanes Oxley Act of 2002 is driving the need for increasing transparency in documenting business operations. A component of any compliance management plan is to document how the business operates today, and where key points of control exist.
    • Auditors and Inspectors are interested in demonstrated control over changes to the business. They will often want to see that a documented process for modifying business operations exists, and that the organization has a structured approach to rolling out Application Control in a controlled and orderly fashion.
  4. How can I demonstrate that the business processes I have employed are actually being adhered to as documented?
    • Once an organization has defined how things will be done, someone needs to monitor the business to ensure that the implementation of new operating rules is being adhered to. Auditors often seek evidence that the newly-defined rules have been implemented. Simple implementation of appropriate measurements and metrics can solve this problem easily.
