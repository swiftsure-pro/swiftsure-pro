Main Description

What is compliance management?

Compliance management  refers to enhancing an organization's processes for achieving and demonstrating that they satisfy applicable standards, policies, and regulations. For more details on what regulations and standards are, refer to Concept: Regulatory Business Overview.

The chart that follows provides examples of what some of the common regulatory compliance standards organizations might have to address:

Managing compliance effectively requires:

  • Adhering to applicable internal and external policies, regulations, and standards
  • Substantiating that the performance and procedural requirements of standards, policies, and regulations are met:
    • Performance requirements: Demonstrate the ability to deliver functionality or tasks related to a specific policy
    • Procedural requirements: Demonstrate adherence to documented operational process

Compliance and IT: What is the role of IT in compliance management?

Compliance and IT are critically related in two major ways:

  • There is a fundamental link between the validity of a given "transaction" and the applications that process it,
  • There is a fundamental link between data and the software applications that modify, store, and delete this data.

IT processes that are designed to help manage compliance are typically:

  • Traceable
  • Transparent
  • Auditable
  • Repeatable
  • Documented
  • Tamper-resistant

Business controls

A key aspect of achieving Compliant status is to create confidence that internal and external policies, regulations, and standards are being implemented within a development process and across its work products. In practice, Typical Compliance Concerns and Common Compliance Challenges can be addressed by providing legitimate evidence of their implementation to an impartial third party, also known as an auditor.

IT compliance is managed by implementing two different types of Business Controls:

  1. IT process controls: These are controls that apply directly to the IT organization to improve the rigor of the software development process itself. For example, these controls might require that adequate project traceability, reporting, and approval procedures are in place throughout the software lifecycle. This roadmap provides an example of IT process controls for a typical commercial IT software development process. These controls can be modified to reflect the level of rigor defined for a specific software development process. Roadmap: Process Controls Applied to RUP describes the process controls introduced and how they are implemented within RUP.
  2. Application controls: These are controls implemented within corporate software. These controls are, therefore, software requirements and can have regulatory or standards-based significance. For example, a common control that applies to the Human Resources (HR) organization is to “Limit access to classified employee data.” This control is automated by making it a common software requirement for all HR systems. The organization’s applications can thus become more consistent in bringing in the function required to provide such limited access. Because these software requirements are of interest to auditors and other compliance stakeholders, we separate these software requirements for unique reporting and traceability processes. We refer to the incorporation of these additional controls into existing systems as “compliance enhancement projects.” Roadmap: Application Controls Development describes how the first roadmap is extended to deal with the development of application controls.
