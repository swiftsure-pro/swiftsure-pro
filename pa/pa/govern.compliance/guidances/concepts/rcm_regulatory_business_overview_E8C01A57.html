Concept: Regulatory Business Overview
This concept provides an introduction to regulations and their varying impact on businesses.
Relationships
Related Elements
  • Compliance Analyst
  • Corporate Compliance Policy
Main Description

When asked the question, “Do you operate in a regulated industry?” many businesses will respond with a resounding “No.” However, the reality is that nearly all businesses operating in the United States and abroad are directly or indirectly influenced by government regulations. For example, in the United States, all businesses must comply with Occupational Safety & Health Administration (OSHA) regulations. In Europe, the European Agency for Safety and Health at Work (EASHW) provides similar regulations. Generally, it is very likely that any business operating in a country with a structured form of government is subject to one or more regulations.

Recognition of whether a business is considered regulated or not more often has to do with the impact that the regulations have on the bottom line than whether the regulations exist. As regulations become part of standard operating procedures within an industry, the impact of these regulations is easily overlooked. For example, if you walk through a mall or restaurant in the U.S. around closing time, you may notice a familiar “Wet Floor” warning sign. Although these signs originated in response to OSHA regulations, today they are typically regarded as a standard operating procedure and a cost of doing business.

Different business domains pose different levels of risk to the general population. Situations that pose risks to life and limb are regulated differently than situations that do not. Where risk is higher, regulations tend to:

  • Set higher bars for compliance and, usually, mean associated higher cost of implementation
  • Demand a stricter standard of accountability and stiffer penalties, when applicable

Regulations often share the following characteristics:

  • Mandatory: Regulatory authorities may require qualification and adherence to specific standards, and they may dictate explicit penalties for noncompliance.
  • Nondirective: While regulators typically define what needs to be done, they do not always detail the how: The specific steps that companies must take to ensure compliance. The interpretation of regulations into specific policies and operating procedures can be left to organizations and their legal and policy-making staffs.
  • Continuously changing: Regulations, their interpretation, and their embodiment in corporate policies can change frequently and at inconvenient times. Commonly accepted standards for meeting regulatory requirements evolve as regulations are tested and their interpretations refined in courts of law. Also, regulatory authorities themselves are malleable. For example, when the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) of the United States developed guidance governing the implementation of the Title 21-Part 11 Electronic Records and Signatures Rule, guidance was developed and revoked four times before the agency settled on a final direction.
  • Increasing IT impact: While few apply directly to IT, regulations increasingly affect IT systems.
  • Non-certifiable: Agencies generally do not approve, recommend, or validate particular solutions. Most agencies will not certify that any particular solution guarantees compliance.
  • Related to risk: Many regulatory agencies acknowledge that a compliance management approach based on examining every detail can be prohibitively expensive. These agencies recommend a risk-based approach to compliance management: This approach is based on performing a thorough assessment of the risks involved in the business and demonstrating that the these risks are being addressed adequately. This allows an organization to establish business controls and conduct internal audits based on the risks identified. As a result, the business processes associated with the greatest degree of risk of noncompliance are the ones managed most proactively.
