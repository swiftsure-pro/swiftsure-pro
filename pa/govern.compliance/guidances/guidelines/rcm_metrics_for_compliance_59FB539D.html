Introduction

Product development processes are considered effective when they yield the desired results for product quality and customer and stakeholder satisfaction. To monitor process effectiveness, the project must establish baselines, collect metrics over time, and use these to evaluate trends (and take corrective or preventive actions, as needed).

Metrics can be very useful in monitoring the progression towards, and adherence to, compliance activities. Objectives of compliance metrics include:

  • Verifying that compliance projects are following the process
  • Demonstrating that the process is working properly, and that it is effective
  • Providing the business with information about project status

Each process workflow should include work products, states, control points, and exceptions. Metrics that measure compliance, and metrics that measure the status and business goals of a project, may be the same or different.

Project Metrics

The list below provides a basic set of common project metrics. These are typically used to measure against status and business goals.

  • Project cost and schedule
    • Cost and schedule variance
  • Defect ratios
    • Defects per Unit of Work
  • Use case management
    • Use cases analyzed, designed, tested, and passed
  • Test case management
    • Test cases analyzed, designed, tested, and passed

Additional Control Metrics

It is key for projects to have the ability to generate specific control metrics. Examples include the ability to:

  • Run a query that follows the process flow of a work product, and validates that each state of the process was successfully completed
  • Run a global query that determines the number of work products in process (and completed) in each state of the process
  • Run a global query that identifies exceptions, and the work products involved in each exception
  • Identify traceability of a work product across the lifecycle (for example, test coverage of requirements).

For further guidance on measurements, please consult the Practical Software Measurement (CMU/SEI 97-HB-003 April 1997)

Collecting Metrics

When you use process effectiveness metrics, your project team should determine how you will record that metric, how often you will record it, and where you will keep the records. You may obtain some of these metrics, such as number of formal test defect escapes, through the tools that are used to support a process. You may obtain others, such as customer satisfaction levels, from other organizations for your use. If there are metrics that your project manually collects, you should take care to determine the metric values in a consistent manner from period to period (and from release to release) to ensure valid comparisons.

How frequently you collect data depends on the process you are analyzing, the duration of that process, and data availability. If your project wants to detect in-process trends, a frequency of weekly or monthly metrics might be appropriate. Otherwise, collecting data once after a release is made generally available might be appropriate, followed by monthly collection of post-deployment data.
