Main Description

Approvals are the method by which acceptance actions are sanctioned. Approvals are put in place to prevent the introduction of unauthorized changes, and they contribute to monitoring the health of a project. They ensure accountability.

Approvals must be documented for auditability purposes. They can be kept through:

  • Either paper based sign-offs: While acceptable, this mechanism is becoming increasingly rare for cost and reliability reasons. It is reserved for the simplest of situations, where approvals are few and the project team is co-located.
  • Or electronic signatures: This is the prevailing mechanism for state-of-the-art compliance management, and it is strongly encouraged by many regulations, including, for example, the FDA.

RUP for Compliance Management distinguishes between two kinds of approvals:

  • Outcome: Work Product Approval: These approvals are intended to confirm that the contents of one or more work products are acceptable and were created according to the development process
  • Outcome: Control Point: These approvals do not specifically address the content of individual work products. They are designed to assess the overall health of the project (to answer the question: "Is the project on track?") at key milestones or handover points. Control points thus gauge the ability of the project to move forward towards completion.

Approval examples:

  • Approval of the test plan: Does the plan provide appropriate test coverage and address the requirements?
  • Approval of a change request by the Change Control Board (CCB) for implementation

Control Point example:

  • Approval of the release of the updated software into a production environment
