Main Description

Audit and Inspections are generally carried out by exception. If a problem is identified, then an investigation commences. Regular audits should be anticipated, prepared for, and scheduled. However, those audits are not likely to be the most stressful ones. It is the unexpected exceptions that cause the most anxiety. During an inspection that results from something like this, auditors or inspectors will look for the loose thread in the traceability chain and begin pulling on that thread until the whole thing unravels.

They may also look at product quality and business process control metrics as indications of process conformance and consistency. Typically, inspectors are looking for one of three things. Was the exception the result of:

  1. System design flaw?
  2. Human error?
  3. Malicious behavior?

Examination for a system design flaw will begin with an investigation centered on the business process artifacts or software artifacts themselves, and then flow to the other parts of the system, process, or other artifacts.

Human error investigations will focus on executing the business process and training individuals, as well as the processes surrounding a target system associated with the failure.

Malicious behavior investigations will evaluate if personal gain could be realized through use or misuse of the business or automated system. Alternatively, loopholes in system behavior will be examined and, of course, a critical examination of the business controls will be employed.

The only way to be prepared for an audit is to have appropriate metrics and documentation, and always have them ready. The potential areas to be covered in such an audit include:

  1. Policy conformance and attestation
  2. Corrective action 
  3. Compliance risk analysis and business controls criteria
  4. Current status on any compliance enhancement task
  5. Projected delivery date for the compliance enhancement project
  6. Application compliance enhancement project health and status
  7. Access to any compliance enhancement design artifact

Manual versus automated auditing

In manual systems, auditing is difficult to achieve. This is due to the labor-intensive nature of the inevitable archeological dig and the associated costs in maintaining the paperwork. Using tool-based solutions can be very beneficial. Artifact linkages occur naturally, quickly, and easily due to Tool Directed Behavior. Another advantage of a tool-based solution is the ability to reach into these artifact repositories for gathering metrics and reporting. Last but not least, tooling makes it possible to always have documentation close at hand.

Through the use of automated report generation tooling, it is possible to construct formalized document templates that meet or exceed reporting requirements for nearly any regulation. These document templates (in conjunction with data stored in the repositories of associated tooling) can be used to generate the documentation necessary to conduct revalidation of the system, examine detailed artifacts, and check the chain of accountability or any other tool-directed linkage established between these artifacts. This provides a method for providing all compliance-related information in a paper-based, human-readable form, as required by most federal agencies today.
