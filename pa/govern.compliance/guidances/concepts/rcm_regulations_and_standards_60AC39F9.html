Concept: Regulations and Standards
This concept illustrates the complementary roles of standards and regulations.
Relationships
Related Elements
  • Compliance Analyst
  • Corporate Compliance Policy
Main Description

Most regulations are nondirective. That means that, while the regulation tells you what you need to do, it does not tell you how to do it.

For example, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, in the United States, requires that CEOs attest to the accuracy of quarterly and annual financial statements. But what does “accuracy” really mean? How far do companies have to go to verify that their financial statements are truthful and accurate? How can they demonstrate that? Here are a few examples:

  • Revenue was accounted for properly
  • Only authorized personnel had access to financial applications
  • All changes to financial applications were made in a secure and controlled environment

Organizations fail audits because their financial applications were improperly managed. Yet what does a “properly managed” financial application look like?

Organizations adopt governance frameworks, such as ITIL, CMMI, and COBIT, to help answer these questions. These frameworks provide guidance in the form of an independent, accredited yardstick by which organizations can implement common measurement and control mechanisms to document compliance. A high degree of commonality exists across all of these standards. For example, there are common control mechanisms that govern the completeness, accuracy, validity, authorization, and segregation of duties with respect to software applications.

The specific framework chosen is less important than the fact that the choice is documented and compliance against the framework is actively managed. In practice, large organizations implement a mix of these frameworks, drawing from ITIL and COBIT, for example. There is often a layering of these frameworks also. For example, organizations may adopt ITIL (the IT Infrastructure Library) to govern their operations environment, COBIT (Control Objectives for Information and Related Technology) to manage their IT governance efforts, and CMMI (the Capability Maturity Model Implementation) to govern their software development organization.
